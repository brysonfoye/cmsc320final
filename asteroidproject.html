Introduction¶

Asteroids. The abundance of leftover rocks from the formation of our solar system approximately 4.6 billion years ago has been discovered and studied by astronomers for centuries. Some Background Information on Asteroids.¶

The definition of an asteroid is a small, rocky or metallic body astrological body that orbits around the Sun. Most asteroids discovered are found in our Asteroid Belt. The amount of time it takes for an asteroid to orbit around the Sun is called the period, measured in our dataset as years. and the distance or mean distance from the asteroid to the Sun is called the semi-major axis, measured in our dataset as Astronomical Units, which is the distance from Earth to the Sun as a unit. The eccentricity of asteroid is how eccentric the asteroid's orbit is. It is measured from 0 to 1, with a measurement closer to 0 being more circular and closer to 1 being more like an ovular shape. These properties of asteroids are what we will be focusing on in this dataset.

These will be the packages used in the project. Pandas for curation and manipulation of the dataframes, Numpy for possible use of arrays for our dataframes, matplotlib for data visualization and sklearn for linear regression of data.

Data Exploration and Visualization¶

Column Representations¶

Before we move on to visualization, I will have what out column names define here for future reference.

  • full_name - Shows asteroid name as well as designation
  • eccentricity - A measurement of the shape of the asteroids orbit from 0 to 1, closer to 0 being near circular and closer to 1 being near an elipse
  • smi-mjr_axis - The semi-major axis. Distance between the asteroid and the Sun measured in Astronomical Units (AU).
  • period_years - The time it takes for the asteroid to orbit around the Sun once. Measured in years.
  • first_obs - Does NOT contain the first discovery of these asteroids, but the date it was first observed. For example, Ceres was first discovered in 1801, but the first observation year was in 1995.

Visualization of Kirkwood Gaps¶

Alright. Now lets get into some plotting. I go ahead and plot a bar graph of the semi-major axes of the asteroids to show the the frequency of asteroids on certain semi-major axes

In [8]:
asteroiddata2['smi-mjr_axis'] = asteroiddata2['smi-mjr_axis'].round(2)
smaplot = asteroiddata2['smi-mjr_axis'].value_counts().sort_index().plot.bar(figsize = (5, 5), title = 'Frequency of Asteroids on Semi-major-Axis') 
#grabbing the frequency of asteroids on semi-major axis, and then sorting the index, which is where the semi-major axis lies, and finally plotting the result.
smaplot.xaxis.set_major_locator(plt.MaxNLocator(5)) #Evenly disperse a set number of ticks across the x axis, as to not fill the x axis with over 10,000 tick lables

Awesome. If we look at the graph above, we can see how the frequency of asteroids spike within certain ranges of semi-major axes, but divit doown between these spikes. These little gaps in frequencies are called the Kirkwood Gaps, and they exist because asteroids that may have previously been at or around those semi-major axis were associated with a certain period that caused them to be in orbital resonance with Jupiter. This means that those asteroids's periods were a simple fraction of the orbital period of Jupiter, which caused them to be either stable in that semi-major axis or nudged away from that axis.

Visualization of Small Object Classes of Asteroids¶

We'll now scatterplot a graph that shows semi-major axis vs eccentricity and see if we can determine certain classes of these asteroids through the trends that show on the graph.

In [9]:
aveplot = asteroiddata2.plot.scatter(x = 'smi-mjr_axis', y = 'eccentricity', title = 'Distribution of Asteroids Eccentricity conditioned on Semi-major Axis')

Weird looking graph, right? However, this graph actually does determine certain small oject classes of the asteroids. The concentrated group of asteroids on the left side of the plot from an eccentricity of near 0 to approx .9 and AUs span from near 0 to 20 are in a class called Plutinos, which are asteroids that orbit two times around the Sunun every three times Neptune orbits the Sun. The slight cluster of asteroids that span 20 to 60 AU and have an eccentricity from 0 to .4 are called Classical objects. There is a class for the two asteroids that lie alone on the top right of the plot called Scattered Disk Objects. There are not many of these kinds of asteroids recorded because of their AU's being too far from Earth and their high eccentricities making it impossible for them to become near the Earth for us to observe easier.

Observing Possible Trends of Diameter of Asteroids conditioned on Discovery¶

For this graph, we will use our dataframe that contains the asteroids with recorded diameters. There is a common misconception by people getting into astronomy that the order of names for the asteroids are determined by size. This is not true! The order of names are determined by their date of discovery. However, many large asteroids are the first few on the graph because their size made it more likely for astronomers to discover earlier in history. Lets see if this relationship between size and order of discover pertains in later discovered asteroids!
